Where it all began...


The very first idea was envisioned by a psychologist and computer scientist J C R Licklider, who conceptualized that if computers communicate better with each other, it will therefore make it better for humans to connect and share information.


Leonard Kleinrock theorized the foundations of communication in computer networking with his queuing theory where small packets of information was passed through the computer network mesh from one node to another. Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) combined their interface messaging processor and packet switching communication system allowing multiple computers to communicate to each other on a single network.


In the years that followed, engineers Vinton Cerf (also known as 'Father of the internet') and Robert Cahn developed a communication model that standardized data transmission through multiple networks, they called it the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) and Internet Protocol (IP). Then further with the help of the English computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee the World Wide Web came into existence, this software made it easy to follow threads of knowledge on the internet without being a computer whiz. And by early 1990s there were approximately 50 functioning websites on the internet.


Simply explained it is a tangle physical system made to move information from point A to point B. This information is chopped up into small pieces and each of this piece has a header that indicates the deriving and receiving destination points. The moving of these data enables us to chat, browse and share content.
